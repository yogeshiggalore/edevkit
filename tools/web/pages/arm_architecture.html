ARM architecture & the Cortex-M family

A first-principles reference for everyone touching the edevkit codebase. The product family is built on Arm Cortex-M cores (edevkit1 uses the Cortex-M33 in the RP2350B); this document is the shared grounding for that work. It explains where ARM came from, what "ARM" actually is as opposed to a chip, how the Cortex-M cores differ, and the architectural pieces — registers, exceptions, memory model, MPU, debug — that every line of firmware ultimately rests on.

Audience: two kinds of reader. (a) Someone who has never written ARM firmware and needs a path from zero. (b) Someone fluent in another architecture (8051, AVR, RISC-V, x86) who needs a comprehensive Cortex-M reference. Every section explains the why alongside the what.

A short history of ARM

The ARM architecture is the most-shipped instruction set in human history. Roughly a quarter of a trillion ARM-based chips have been built, and almost every microcontroller in a modern product — wearable, car, sensor, dishwasher, toy, satellite — is some flavour of ARM. None of that was inevitable. The story starts with a small British education-computer company that needed a CPU nobody would sell them.

Acorn and the BBC Micro (1978–1983)

Acorn Computers, based in Cambridge, England, built the BBC Micro in 1981 — a 6502-based home/education machine commissioned by the BBC for its Computer Literacy Project. The BBC Micro shipped roughly 1.5 million units and dominated UK schools through the 1980s. Acorn's engineers liked the 6502: simple, predictable, fast for its clock. But by 1983 the 6502 was running out of headroom, and the obvious successors — Motorola 68000, Intel 80286, National Semiconductor 32016 — were either too slow under the kinds of memory Acorn could afford, or too expensive, or both.

Two things happened around that time that pushed Acorn into building its own CPU. First, Steve Furber and Sophie Wilson had visited the Western Design Center in Phoenix, Arizona — where the 6502 was made — and had been struck by how small the 6502 design team was. Two engineers and a handful of test chairs were producing the chip Acorn was buying. Second, papers out of Berkeley (Patterson) and Stanford (Hennessy) were articulating the RISC philosophy: small, fixed-width, load/store instruction sets could outperform big CISC machines on real workloads. Wilson and Furber concluded that if two people could build the 6502, two people could build a RISC follow-up.

ARM1 (1985)

The Acorn RISC Machine project formally started in October 1983. Wilson designed the instruction set; Furber led the implementation. Critically the team had almost no money for simulation, so the architecture was kept ruthlessly simple — 26-bit addressing, fixed 32-bit instructions, three-stage pipeline (fetch / decode / execute), every instruction conditional. The first silicon, ARM1, came back from VLSI Technology and worked correctly the first time it was tested — on 26 April 1985, a date now famous in chip-design folklore. ARM1 ran at 6 MHz and was used internally as a coprocessor card for the BBC Micro.

ARM2, ARM3, and the Archimedes (1986–1989)

ARM2 (late 1986, 8 MHz; early 1987, 10–12 MHz) was the first commercial ARM. It powered the Acorn Archimedes, launched on 6 June 1987 — the first home computer with a RISC CPU. The Archimedes was technically remarkable (its Dhrystone numbers crushed the 386) and commercially marginal: it sold well into UK schools but never broke into the wider PC market that IBM-compatibles already owned.

What mattered long-term was that ARM's architecture had been designed under a constraint Wilson and Furber didn't fully realise at the time: extreme power efficiency. The original chip didn't even have a VDD ball wired correctly on one early prototype, yet still booted — it was running off the bus's leakage current. RISC simplicity, low transistor count, and a focus on real-world workloads gave ARM a profile (mips-per-milliwatt) that nobody else had.

Advanced RISC Machines Ltd (1990)

By 1990 Apple was looking for a CPU for its Newton handheld project. Apple's specifications — battery-powered, fits in a pocket, runs handwriting recognition — were exactly the workload ARM was best at, and exactly the kind no other 1990 CPU could touch. Apple, Acorn, and chip-fab partner VLSI Technology spun the ARM design team out as a joint venture: Advanced RISC Machines Ltd, formally registered 12 November 1990 (initially "Widelogic Limited", renamed 3 December 1990). Acorn contributed 12 employees, VLSI contributed tools, Apple contributed roughly US$3 million in cash. The new company had a single business model that nobody had really tried before: license the design, don't build the chip. ARM Ltd would design CPUs and license the IP to anyone willing to pay; partners would build their own SoCs.

The first product of the new company was ARM6 (1992), shipped as the ARM610 in the Apple Newton MessagePad in 1993. The Newton failed commercially, but ARM6 was solid silicon and the licensing model started attracting customers — DEC licensed ARMv4 to build StrongARM (1995), then later Intel acquired StrongARM and turned it into XScale.

ARM7TDMI — the chip that conquered the 1990s (1994)

The ARM7TDMI (1994) is arguably the most important embedded CPU ever built. The "TDMI" letters stand for Thumb (16-bit compressed instructions), Debug (JTAG), Multiplier (32×32→64), ICE (in-circuit emulation). Three-stage pipeline, ARMv4T architecture, vanishingly small die area, and — crucially — the new Thumb instruction set, a 16-bit re-encoding of a subset of ARM that halved code size for memory-constrained systems. ARM7TDMI ended up inside:

  • The Game Boy Advance (2001) — Nintendo's best-selling handheld until the DS.
  • Early iPod models — Apple's reentry into consumer electronics.
  • Most Nokia feature phones of the 2000s, and many Ericsson and Motorola models.
  • Countless industrial controllers, modems, printers, and white goods.

By the late 1990s "ARM" had stopped being an Acorn-specific brand and become the default CPU for any battery-powered device.

IPO and the licensing flywheel (1998)

ARM Ltd floated on the London Stock Exchange and Nasdaq in 1998, simultaneously becoming "ARM Holdings plc". Revenue model: a one-time licence fee per design (millions of dollars) plus a per-chip royalty (cents). Because designs are reused for years and royalties compound across billions of chips, the model became a flywheel — every new licensee was nearly pure margin once the IP was finished. Through the 2000s ARM acquired competitors (the 8051 alternative Keil Software, debug-tools firm Lauterbach's rival lines, etc.) and consolidated the embedded software stack around its cores.

The Cortex split (2004)

By 2004 ARM had three distinct customer profiles trying to use one architecture: Linux-class application processors (mobile phones), real-time-control systems (automotive, industrial), and microcontrollers (low-power, deterministic). One ISA couldn't serve all three well — application processors wanted MMUs and Linux; MCUs wanted tiny cores with deterministic interrupt latency; real-time wanted both at once. ARM announced the Cortex family with three profiles:

  • Cortex-A — Application processors with MMUs, caches, multi-issue, big-OS support.
  • Cortex-R — Real-time controllers with MPU, low-jitter interrupts, but enough ALU power for storage controllers and modems.
  • Cortex-M — Microcontrollers: deterministic, low-power, NVIC-driven, no MMU, often no FPU. Bare-metal or RTOS only.

Cortex-M3 was the first M-profile core, announced in 2004 and shipping in silicon by 2006 (in NXP's LPC1768 and ST's STM32F1, among others). It introduced the ARMv7-M architecture, the Thumb-2 mixed-width instruction set, the NVIC, and the basic exception model that every later Cortex-M still uses. From here on, the rest of this document is mostly Cortex-M.

Thumb-2 — the unification (2003–2006)

Original Thumb (1994) was a 16-bit-only re-encoding of a subset of ARM. Code was smaller, but you couldn't write a whole program in Thumb — anything sufficiently complex had to drop back to 32-bit ARM, with mode-switch instructions and constant overhead. Thumb-2 (introduced in ARMv6T2, fully exploited in ARMv7-M and ARMv7-A) added 32-bit Thumb instructions that mix freely with 16-bit ones, so a program can be encoded entirely in Thumb-2 with the size advantage of 16-bit-where-possible and the power of 32-bit-where-needed. Cortex-M cores went one step further: they execute only Thumb-2 — there is no 32-bit-ARM mode at all. The T bit in the EPSR is hardwired to 1, and writing 0 to it triggers a fault.

SoftBank acquisition (2016)

On 5 September 2016, SoftBank Group announced it would acquire ARM Holdings for approximately £24.3 billion (about US$32 billion at the time) — the largest-ever acquisition of a European tech company. ARM was delisted and became a SoftBank subsidiary. Strategically, SoftBank framed the buy as a bet on the "Internet of Things": every device would have a CPU, every CPU would be ARM. Operationally, the licensing flywheel continued unchanged.

The Nvidia attempt (2020–2022)

In September 2020, Nvidia announced it would acquire ARM from SoftBank for ~US$40 billion. The deal collapsed in February 2022 under regulatory pressure: ARM's neutrality (it licenses to everyone, including Nvidia's competitors) was seen as critical to the entire semiconductor industry, and selling it to one of those competitors raised antitrust alarms across multiple jurisdictions.

Second IPO and "Arm" rebrand (2017, 2023)

ARM rebranded internally to lowercase "Arm" on 1 August 2017 — a small but signalled shift toward consumer-facing branding. On 14 September 2023, Arm Holdings IPO'd again, this time on Nasdaq, raising US$4.87 billion at a $54.5 billion valuation. SoftBank retains roughly 90% of shares.

Architecture vs. implementation: the three profiles

One of the easiest ways to confuse yourself when first encountering ARM is conflating "the ARM architecture" with "an ARM chip". They're very different things, and almost every Cortex-M document you'll read assumes you understand the distinction. So:

key idea "ARM" is an architecture — an instruction set + memory model + exception model — that is licensed to chip vendors. A "Cortex-M3" is an implementation of that architecture: a specific RTL design that vendors integrate into their SoCs. An "STM32F103" is a vendor SoC: it contains a Cortex-M3 plus ST's peripherals, clocks, flash, and pinout. Three layers, three different kinds of document.

This separation is why a piece of code that compiles for an STM32F103 (Cortex-M3) will mostly run on an LPC1768 (also Cortex-M3) or an SAM3X8E (also Cortex-M3) without source changes — the ARM core is identical, only the peripherals around it differ. The skill of an embedded engineer is partly knowing which bits of your firmware are "talking to the ARM core" (portable across vendors) and which are "talking to the SoC peripherals" (vendor-specific).

The three Cortex profiles

Since 2004, ARM has organised its 32-bit (and now 64-bit) cores into three profiles. They share a common heritage, but they are emphatically different ISAs: a Cortex-A program will not run on a Cortex-M and vice versa.

Why three profiles?

The choices that make a good application processor make a bad microcontroller, and vice versa. An MMU adds dozens of cycles to every memory access (TLB walks, page-table reads); that's fine when you have an L1 cache hiding the latency, but it kills determinism in an interrupt handler. Caches improve average-case latency but destroy worst-case latency — the very thing a motor controller cares about. Big OS support requires user/kernel separation and trapping system calls; in an MCU you'd prefer the system call to be a function call.

The three profiles are essentially three points on a curve from "best at running Linux" to "best at responding to a hardware interrupt in 12 cycles". For edevkit, you only need to think about Cortex-M from here on.

Cortex-M defining properties

  • Thumb-2 only. No 32-bit ARM mode. Every instruction is encoded in Thumb-2 (16 or 32 bits).
  • NVIC, not GIC. The Nested Vectored Interrupt Controller is part of the core, tightly integrated, and gives you 12-cycle interrupt entry. Cortex-A uses the much larger Generic Interrupt Controller (GIC).
  • Hardware exception stacking. On exception entry the core auto-pushes 8 registers (R0–R3, R12, LR, PC, xPSR). This means the very first instruction of an ISR can be C — no assembly prologue required.
  • Vector table is a list of addresses, not jumps. Each entry is a 32-bit pointer to a handler. Cortex-A's vector table is a list of instructions (usually a branch); Cortex-M is a list of function pointers. This matters when you're writing one in C.
  • No MMU. Physical addresses everywhere. The MPU (when present) is access-control only; it does not translate.
  • Single address space, 4 GB flat. Code, data, peripherals, and debug all in the same 32-bit address space, with a fixed default partitioning (see §Memory model).

A note on Thumb-2 — why it changed everything for MCUs

Thumb-2 is the single instruction-set development that defines modern Cortex-M. To understand why, consider a typical microcontroller program: lots of bit-twiddling, lots of pointer arithmetic, lots of conditional branches, occasional 32-bit math. A pure 32-bit ARM encoding wastes flash on the easy cases (loading a small constant, branching to a nearby label, masking a single bit). A pure 16-bit Thumb-1 encoding can't express the hard cases (32-bit immediates, full-range branches, IT-block conditionals).

Thumb-2 mixes them: a compiler emits 16-bit forms for the common case and 32-bit forms for the harder cases. Net result is roughly 30% smaller code than ARM with no measurable performance loss. Cortex-M cores execute only Thumb-2 — the hardware is simpler because it doesn't have the ARM-mode pipeline; the EPSR's T bit is hardwired to 1; trying to clear it (e.g. by branching to an even address) generates a UsageFault.

Helium / MVE (ARMv8.1-M)

Helium, formally the M-Profile Vector Extension (MVE), is a SIMD/vector instruction set added in ARMv8.1-M (2019). It's roughly analogous to NEON on Cortex-A but designed under MCU constraints — the vector registers share storage with the FPU register file rather than adding a third register file, which keeps die area small. Cortex-M55 was the first core to ship it; M52 and M85 followed. Helium accelerates audio, image, and ML inference on MCUs and is the reason "TinyML on a Cortex-M" became a category in the early 2020s. The Cortex-M33 in edevkit1's RP2350B does not have Helium — it predates the extension.

TrustZone-M (ARMv8-M)

TrustZone-M (introduced in ARMv8-M, 2016) splits the address space into Secure and Non-secure regions, with hardware-enforced transitions. This is different from TrustZone-A (which splits into Secure and Non-secure worlds using a separate MMU); TrustZone-M is much lighter — it adds a Security Attribution Unit (SAU), reserves part of the NVIC priorities for Secure interrupts, and provides Secure Gateway (SG) instructions for controlled cross-world calls. Common use cases: keep cryptographic keys out of application code, isolate a measured-boot stage from the rest of the app, run a TF-M (Trusted Firmware-M) reference stack alongside an RTOS. TrustZone-M is optional on every M-profile core; it's present on RP2350B's M33 but edevkit1 currently builds with TrustZone disabled (single-world boot).

Cortex-M0 (2009)

The smallest mainstream ARM core ever shipped — about 12,000 gates in its smallest configuration. Designed to displace 8-bit MCUs (8051, AVR, PIC) in cost-sensitive applications. ARMv6-M, 3-stage pipeline, 56 base instructions, Thumb-1 plus a small Thumb-2 subset (BL, DMB, DSB, ISB, MRS, MSR). No DSP, no FPU, no divide, no IT block. NVIC supports up to 32 external interrupts. Used in Nordic nRF51, NXP LPC11xx, ST STM32F0.

Cortex-M0+ (2012)

An optimised re-implementation of M0: shorter pipeline (2 stages), single-cycle GPIO option, optional MPU. Lower power than M0, same ISA. The most commonly seen "tiny" Cortex-M today: NXP Kinetis KL series, Microchip SAM L21, RP2040 (the first Pi Pico chip). Note: edevkit1's predecessor target had RP2040 (M0+); edevkit1 itself moved to RP2350B (dual M33).

Cortex-M1 (2007)

An FPGA-soft core, sold to FPGA vendors (originally Actel/Microsemi, later Altera, Xilinx). Same ARMv6-M ISA as M0/M0+ but optimised for FPGA fabric rather than ASIC. Niche today; you'll meet it only if you're working with older FPGA SoC platforms.

Cortex-M3 (2004)

The first M-profile core, and the workhorse of the 2010s. ARMv7-M, full Thumb-2, hardware divide, saturated arithmetic, optional MPU, NVIC up to 240 interrupts. Three-stage pipeline. The ST STM32F1, NXP LPC1768, TI Stellaris LM3S, and Atmel SAM3 ranges all built their early lineups on M3. Most introductory-Cortex-M textbooks (Yiu's Cortex-M3 Definitive Guide) target this core.

Cortex-M4 (2010)

M3 plus the DSP extensions of ARMv7E-M (single-cycle MAC, packed SIMD on 32-bit registers, saturating add/sub) and an optional single-precision FPU. Aimed at signal processing — audio codecs, motor-control loops, sensor fusion. STM32F4, NXP K64, Nordic nRF52840 (M4F = M4 with FPU). For most "I want decent compute on a battery" products through the late 2010s, M4F was the default answer.

Cortex-M7 (2014)

The first high-performance Cortex-M. 6-stage in-order superscalar (dual-issue under restricted conditions), branch prediction, optional caches (up to 64 KB I and 64 KB D), TCM (Tightly Coupled Memory) interfaces, optional double-precision FPU. STM32H7 family hits 480 MHz. Targeted at applications previously served by Cortex-A or DSP — high-end motor control, audio mixing, machine vision. Caveat: M7 introduces cache coherency complications that lower-end M cores don't have, and you'll want to read the cache-management chapter of the TRM before doing DMA.

Cortex-M23 (2016)

The ARMv8-M Baseline core. Roughly "M0+ with TrustZone-M added". 2-stage pipeline, very small die area, hardware divide, compare-and-branch (CBZ/CBNZ). Targeted at IoT endpoints where the security uplift from TrustZone-M is needed but the budget for full M33 isn't. Microchip SAM L11, Nuvoton M2351.

Cortex-M33 (2016)

The ARMv8-M Mainline core. Roughly "M4 with TrustZone-M". 3-stage pipeline, optional FPU, optional DSP, optional MPU, optional TrustZone, optional co-processor interface. ST STM32L5 / U5 / H5, Nordic nRF53/nRF54, NXP LPC55xx, Renesas RA, Raspberry Pi RP2350. This is the core in edevkit1's RP2350B — see arm_cortex_m33.html for the deep dive.

Cortex-M35P (2018)

M33 plus optional anti-tamper / side-channel protections (the "P" stands for "physical security"). Adds a parity/error-correcting cache and design-time mitigations against power-analysis attacks. Used in secure-element-class chips and smart-card SoCs.

Cortex-M52 (2023)

The most recent M-profile addition (announced April 2024 for ARMv8.1-M). Targets cost-sensitive ML / DSP — Helium with MVE-int but optional MVE-fp; lower area and power than M55. Renesas RA8T1 was an early licensee.

Cortex-M55 (2020)

First Cortex-M with Helium / MVE. ARMv8.1-M Mainline, 4–5-stage pipeline, optional FPU, optional caches. Designed for "ML on the edge" — keyword-spotting, sensor fusion, vision pipelines. Helium gives ~5–15× speedup over M4 on typical DSP/ML kernels.

Cortex-M85 (2022)

The current Cortex-M performance flagship. 7-stage dual-issue pipeline, Helium, optional FPU/caches, PAC (Pointer Authentication) and BTI (Branch Target Identification) for control-flow integrity. Renesas RA8M1 was an early lead chip. Aimed squarely at workloads previously needing a Cortex-A — small-screen UIs, edge-AI inference, software-defined radio.

Programmer's model — registers, modes, privilege

Every Cortex-M core (M0 through M85) shares the same programmer-visible model: 16 32-bit "main" registers, a small set of special-purpose registers, two operating modes, two privilege levels, and (since ARMv8-M) optional Secure / Non-secure banking on top. Most of what you'll do in firmware ultimately reads and writes these registers, even when you're really writing C — the compiler is just a translator.

R0–R12 are general-purpose 32-bit registers. R13 is the stack pointer, but it is banked — there are physically two stack pointers, MSP and PSP, and which one the name "R13" refers to depends on the current execution context (more on this below). R14 is the link register: when you execute a BL (branch-and-link) instruction, the return address is stored in LR. R15 is the program counter; in Thumb-2, reading PC always returns the address of the current instruction + 4 (a quirk inherited from the original three-stage pipeline).

AAPCS calling convention C compilers for ARM follow the AAPCS (Procedure Call Standard for the Arm Architecture). R0–R3 hold function arguments (and R0 the return value); R4–R11 are callee-saved; R12 is scratch (a.k.a. IP, the "intra-procedure-call scratch register"); LR holds the return address; SP is 8-byte aligned at function-call boundaries. When you read disassembly and wonder "why is the compiler clobbering R0–R3 so freely?" — that's why.

Banked stack pointers (MSP and PSP)

The "two SPs" thing is one of Cortex-M's defining features. The hardware has two physical 32-bit stack-pointer registers:

  • MSP — Main Stack Pointer. Used at reset, in all exception handlers, and in privileged Thread mode by default. This is the SP your bare-metal main() runs against.
  • PSP — Process Stack Pointer. Used by Thread mode only when an RTOS or scheduler explicitly switches to it (by setting CONTROL.SPSEL = 1). RTOS task stacks live in PSP-space; the kernel and ISRs run on MSP.

The instruction set sees R13 / SP. Which physical register R13 maps to depends on (mode, CONTROL.SPSEL):

ModeCONTROL.SPSELSP refers toTypical use
Handler(forced 0)MSPAll ISRs always run on MSP.
Thread0MSPBare-metal main() before RTOS takes over; or single-stack RTOS configs.
Thread1PSPRTOS task code runs here.

Why bother? Two reasons. (1) Stack overflow isolation: if a task overflows its own stack (PSP), it doesn't corrupt the kernel's stack (MSP). (2) Cleaner context switching: the kernel can save/restore PSP without touching MSP, so an ISR can preempt a task and the task's stack frame stays untouched. If you've used RTOSes on AVR or PIC, this is a feature you've been working without; it's a real quality-of-life upgrade.

The exception model — NVIC, vector table, stacking

An "exception" on Cortex-M means anything that interrupts the linear flow of instructions: hardware interrupts (UART RX, timer overflow, GPIO edge), supervisor calls (SVC), faults (bus error, unaligned access), the periodic SysTick, and so on. Cortex-M handles all of them through one mechanism: the NVIC (Nested Vectored Interrupt Controller). Understanding NVIC is non-negotiable for embedded ARM work.

The vector table

The vector table is a flat array of 32-bit pointers placed at the base of code memory (by default 0x00000000; relocatable via VTOR). Slot 0 is special: it holds the initial value loaded into MSP at reset — not a function pointer. From slot 1 onward each entry is the address of a handler function.

Priority encoding and grouping

NVIC priority is stored in 8-bit fields, but only the top N bits are implemented in silicon — usually 3, 4, or 8 depending on the core and vendor configuration. The Cortex-M3 baseline implements 3 bits → 8 distinct priorities. The Cortex-M33 implementation in RP2350B implements 4 bits → 16 priorities.

NVIC priority byte (8 bits, MSB-aligned)
┌──────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
│  Pre-emption priority    │  Sub-priority            │
│  (split via PRIGROUP)    │  (split via PRIGROUP)    │
└──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┘
   higher byte = LOWER priority (0 = highest urgency)

Two important conventions:

  1. Lower numeric value = higher priority. Priority 0 wins over priority 7. This trips up everyone learning NVIC for the first time.
  2. Only the implemented bits matter. If your chip implements 3 priority bits, the priorities are stored in bits [7:5], so writing 0x80 and 0xA0 both round down to the same priority value internally. CMSIS provides __NVIC_PRIO_BITS macros so portable code can shift correctly.

The SCB->AIRCR.PRIGROUP field splits the 8 bits between pre-emption priority (whether one IRQ can pre-empt another) and sub-priority (tie-breaker for two pending IRQs at the same pre-emption priority).

Exception entry: hardware stacking

When an exception fires, the Cortex-M core does this in hardware, before your handler runs:

  1. Push 8 registers onto the active stack (MSP if from Handler mode or from Thread/MSP, PSP if from Thread/PSP). The order is fixed: 
    SP -> ┌──────────────┐  <-- lower address
       │     R0       │
       │     R1       │
       │     R2       │
       │     R3       │
       │     R12      │
       │     LR       │
       │     PC       │  <-- the address of the *next* instruction the
       │     xPSR     │      interrupted code would have run
       └──────────────┘  <-- higher address
  2. Load LR with EXC_RETURN. A magic value (0xFFFFFFFx) that encodes "this is an exception return" plus which mode/SP to return to. See below.
  3. Fetch the vector from the vector table at offset (4 × exception_number). This is concurrent with stacking — Cortex-M overlaps the two.
  4. Set IPSR to the exception number; switch to Handler mode; SP becomes MSP.
  5. Branch to the handler.

Because R0–R3, R12, LR, PC, and xPSR are all auto-saved, an interrupt handler written in C is correct without any assembly prologue — the C ABI just sees a normal function call where the saved registers are already on the stack. This is why Cortex-M ISRs are written in C with no special attributes. (On AVR or PIC, you have to mark a function __interrupt so the compiler emits a save/restore preamble; on Cortex-M, you don't.)

If the FPU is in use and dirty, an additional 18 words (S0–S15 plus FPSCR plus padding) are stacked — this is the extended stack frame. Cortex-M4F and later support lazy FP stacking: the space is reserved on the stack, but the actual S0–S15 are only saved if the handler itself touches FP, saving cycles in pure-integer ISRs.

Exception return: EXC_RETURN values

When the handler returns (via BX LR, POP {PC}, etc.), the core sees the magic LR value and unstacks. EXC_RETURN encodes:

EXC_RETURNMode after returnSP after returnFrame type

0xFFFFFFF9ThreadMSPBasic

0xFFFFFFE1HandlerMSPExtended (with FP)
0xFFFFFFE9ThreadMSPExtended
0xFFFFFFEDThreadPSPExtended

An RTOS context switch in PendSV typically modifies the EXC_RETURN value before returning — for example, switching from "return to MSP" to "return to PSP" so the next thread runs on its own stack.

Tail-chaining and late-arrival

Two important optimisations the core does automatically — they affect interrupt-latency calculations and the design of priority assignments.

Tail-chaining: if an ISR is about to return (unstacking) and another exception is already pending, the core skips the unstack-then-restack sequence (which would push and pop the same 8 registers) and jumps directly to the next handler with the original frame still on the stack. Saves ~6 cycles per chained IRQ pair.

Late arrival: if a higher-priority exception fires while the core is still stacking for a lower-priority one, the core can switch targets — fetch the higher-priority handler's vector instead, while the same stack frame is reused. The lower-priority IRQ then becomes pending and tail-chains afterwards. Saves the latency of completing the stack-then-unstack pair.

SVC, PendSV, and SysTick — the RTOS triad

Three exceptions exist specifically because RTOSes need them, and almost every Cortex-M RTOS (FreeRTOS, Zephyr, ThreadX, RT-Thread, Mbed) uses them in essentially the same way:

  • SVC (SVCall) — A synchronous, deliberate trap from Thread to Handler mode, invoked by the SVC #imm instruction. Used as the entry into kernel syscalls (e.g. k_yield(), k_sleep()): the user task executes SVC #N, the SVC handler reads the immediate, dispatches the corresponding kernel function. Why use SVC instead of a regular function call? Because SVC promotes execution to privileged mode, so the kernel can do things the unprivileged task can't.
  • PendSV (Pendable SV) — A pending, deferrable exception used universally for context switching. Configured at the lowest priority. When the kernel decides to switch tasks (from a higher-priority ISR, from SVC, from a SysTick tick), it sets SCB->ICSR.PENDSVSET. The PendSV handler then runs after all higher-priority work is done, performs the actual save-old-context / restore-new-context sequence, and returns into the new task. This guarantees task switches happen at a quiet moment, not in the middle of a high-priority IRQ.
  • SysTick — A 24-bit architectural countdown timer baked into every Cortex-M core. The RTOS configures it to fire every 1 ms (or whatever CONFIG_SYS_CLOCK_TICKS_PER_SEC is) to drive scheduling decisions. Because SysTick is part of the core, kernel code that uses it is portable across all Cortex-M vendors.
// Sketch of a PendSV-based context switch (simplified, ARM asm):
PendSV_Handler:
    cpsid  i               // mask interrupts during the swap
    mrs    r0, psp         // r0 = current task's PSP
    stmdb  r0!, {r4-r11}   // push callee-saved (R4-R11) onto its stack

    ldr    r1, =current_tcb
    ldr    r2, [r1]        // r2 = current TCB
    str    r0, [r2]        // save new SP into current TCB

    bl     scheduler_pick_next   // returns next TCB in r0
    str    r0, [r1]              // current_tcb = next

    ldr    r0, [r0]              // r0 = next task's saved SP
    ldmia  r0!, {r4-r11}         // pop R4-R11
    msr    psp, r0               // PSP = top of next task's frame

    cpsie  i
    bx     lr               // hardware unstacks R0-R3,R12,LR,PC,xPSR
                            // EXC_RETURN says "Thread + PSP", so we
                            // return into the next task naturally.

Cortex-M classifies every memory region into one of three types. The classification controls what the bus / cache infrastructure may legally do.

Bit-banding (Cortex-M3 and M4 only)

Two address regions on M3/M4 are aliased so each bit of memory in the source region maps to a 32-bit word in the alias region. Writing to that word atomically sets or clears the corresponding bit. This is the bit-banding region — a feature that lets you do "set bit 7 of GPIO PORTA's data register" without a read-modify-write sequence.

// Bit-banding on Cortex-M3/M4 (NOT present on M0/M0+/M23/M33)
// SRAM bit-band:        0x20000000 -> alias 0x22000000
// Peripheral bit-band:  0x40000000 -> alias 0x42000000

// Address translation:
//   alias = base_alias + (byte_offset * 32) + (bit_pos * 4)

#define BITBAND_PERIPH(addr, bit) \
    (*(volatile uint32_t *) \
     (0x42000000 + (((uint32_t)(addr) - 0x40000000) * 32) + ((bit) * 4)))

BITBAND_PERIPH(&GPIOA->ODR, 5) = 1;   // atomic set of GPIOA pin 5
BITBAND_PERIPH(&GPIOA->ODR, 5) = 0;   // atomic clear of GPIOA pin 5

Bit-banding was removed in ARMv8-M. Cortex-M23, M33, M55, M85 do not have it — too much address-space "waste" in a security-extended world. To do atomic bit operations on those cores, use exclusive load/store (LDREX/STREX) or vendor-specific atomic-set/clear registers (e.g. RP2350's GPIO has SET/CLR/XOR alias registers in the SIO block).

Endianness

Cortex-M cores can be configured for little-endian or "BE-8" (byte-invariant big-endian) at reset; almost every shipped chip is little-endian. Don't assume — but in practice, for embedded ARM, it's safe to assume little-endian unless you've been told otherwise. The endianness is fixed at silicon reset; you cannot switch at runtime.

Memory Protection Unit (MPU)

The MPU is an optional hardware unit (present on most M3/M4/M7/M23/M33/M55/M85 implementations, configurable by the licensee) that lets privileged code mark regions of the address space as readable / writable / executable / cacheable / shareable with finer granularity than the default memory map. Unlike a Cortex-A MMU, the MPU does not translate addresses — it only checks that the current access is permitted; if not, it raises a MemManage fault.

Region model

The Cortex-M MPU exposes a fixed number of regions — usually 8 or 16, vendor-configurable. Each region has:

  • A base address (with size-aligned constraint on v7-M; arbitrary 32-byte-aligned on v8-M).
  • A size (power-of-two 32 B–4 GB on v7-M; 32-byte multiples on v8-M).
  • Access permissions (RWX combinations, separately for privileged vs unprivileged).
  • A memory-type attribute (Normal / Device / SO and cacheability).
  • An "execute never" (XN) bit.
  • A subregion-disable mask (v7-M only; splits a region into 8 equal subregions).

Regions can overlap — higher-numbered regions take priority over lower-numbered ones. The default "background region" (when MPU is enabled but no region matches) can be configured to use the default memory map (privileged-only) or to fault.

When MPU pays for itself

  • RTOS task isolation. Each task gets a region that allows access to its own stack and heap, plus shared regions for kernel data. A bug in one task that writes wild pointers gets caught immediately rather than silently corrupting another task.
  • Stack-overflow detection. Place a 32-byte XN+R0+W0 region just below each task's stack. An overflow raises MemManage at the moment it happens, not later when the corrupted data is used.
  • NULL-pointer dereferences. Mark 0x00000000–0x000000FF as fault-on-access. Any code that does *((uint32_t*)0) traps, even though physically there's flash there.
  • Functional Safety / MISRA / ISO 26262. Required by the standards above for separating mixed-criticality tasks.
When MPU is overkill Single-task firmware (one main loop, one ISR each for the peripherals you use) doesn't need an MPU. Adding one only makes sense once the firmware has multiple tasks at multiple criticalities or you're chasing a particularly nasty bug in a complex codebase. edevkit1's firmware is multi-task (USB stack on core 0, protocol engines on core 1) and will eventually use the MPU for stack-overflow detection per thread; it does not currently.

Configuring an MPU region in CMSIS

// Cortex-M33 / ARMv8-M MPU example (CMSIS-Core)
#include "core_cm33.h"

void mpu_setup(void) {
    ARM_MPU_Disable();

    // Region 0: flash (read+execute, no write)
    ARM_MPU_SetRegion(0,
        ARM_MPU_RBAR(0x00000000UL,
                     ARM_MPU_SH_NON,    // non-shareable
                     1, 1, 0),          // RO, NP-OK, XN-no
        ARM_MPU_RLAR(0x00200000UL - 1, 0));   // up to 2 MB

    // Region 1: SRAM (RW, no execute)
    ARM_MPU_SetRegion(1,
        ARM_MPU_RBAR(0x20000000UL, ARM_MPU_SH_NON, 0, 1, 1),
        ARM_MPU_RLAR(0x20040000UL - 1, 0));

    // Region 2: peripherals (RW, device memory, no execute)
    ARM_MPU_SetRegion(2,
        ARM_MPU_RBAR(0x40000000UL, ARM_MPU_SH_OUTER, 0, 1, 1),
        ARM_MPU_RLAR(0x60000000UL - 1, 1));   // attribute index 1 = Device

    // Enable MPU; default region disabled (any access outside our regions faults)
    ARM_MPU_Enable(MPU_CTRL_PRIVDEFENA_Msk);
}

SysTick — the architectural heartbeat

SysTick is a 24-bit countdown timer baked into every Cortex-M core. It exists for one reason: to give an OS-portable tick source that doesn't depend on any vendor peripheral. Every RTOS port for Cortex-M uses it as its primary tick (FreeRTOS's xPortSysTickHandler, Zephyr's z_arm_systick, Mbed's os_tick).

For most purposes you'll never write SysTick code directly — your RTOS or HAL configures it for you. You'll see it in two situations: (a) writing your own RTOS, in which case you study the SysTick chapter of the Cortex-M Generic User Guide; (b) debugging RTOS tick storms, in which case you check that SYST_RVR and SYST_CSR match what the kernel expects.

Debug architecture — CoreSight, DAP, ITM, DWT, FPB, ETM, SWO

One of the major reasons to choose an ARM core over a discrete 8-bit MCU is the debug architecture. Cortex-M ships with CoreSight, an industry-standard, on-chip, multi-component debug system that gives you halt/run, hardware breakpoints, watchpoints, instruction trace, data trace, and printf-style messaging — all over a 2-pin (SWD) or 5-pin (JTAG) cable. Every modern probe (probe-rs, OpenOCD, J-Link, ST-Link, BlackMagic, CMSIS-DAP, Pi Pico Debug Probe, edevkit1 itself) talks to CoreSight.

CoreSight is modular — chip vendors include the components they want, and the components are connected internally by the ATB (Advanced Trace Bus) for trace data and the APB-D bus for control. The host probe interacts only with the DAP; everything else is driven through DAP transactions.

DAP — Debug Access Port

The DAP is the entry point. The probe sends serial bits in over SWD or JTAG; the DAP turns them into bus transactions on the chip's internal AHB. Two halves:

  • SW-DP / JTAG-DP — the Debug Port, which talks to the probe (Serial Wire Debug Port or JTAG Debug Port). SWD is 2-pin (SWCLK + SWDIO), JTAG is 5-pin (TCK, TMS, TDI, TDO, nTRST). Most modern Cortex-M chips can do both, switchable.
  • AP — Access Port(s). An AP is a slave on the DAP that implements one specific kind of access. AHB-AP gives memory-bus access (read/write any address, the workhorse). APB-AP gives access to the slow APB-D control bus. JTAG-AP gives JTAG-chain access for tap-mounted IP. Most Cortex-M chips have at least an AHB-AP.

The probe's job, fundamentally, is to expose "read 32-bit word at address X" and "write 32-bit word at address X" over USB to the host. Everything else — halt/run, breakpoints, ITM, ETM — is reading and writing specific memory-mapped registers in the PPB through the AHB-AP.

ITM — Instrumentation Trace Macrocell

The ITM is a peripheral inside the PPB at 0xE0000000 that exposes 32 "stimulus ports" (FIFOs) that firmware can write 8/16/32-bit values to. Each write becomes a packet on the trace bus and is forwarded out via SWO or via ETM's TPIU. The host decodes the packets and shows them as a stream.

The most common use: printf-over-SWO. You wire fputc() or _write() to write each character to ITM port 0. The probe captures the stream and displays it in the debugger console. Latency is microseconds, throughput is up to a few Mbps with a fast trace clock — far better than a UART, and uses a single chip pin.

// Minimal "printf over ITM" using CMSIS-Core
#include "core_cm33.h"

int _write(int file, const char *ptr, int len) {
    for (int i = 0; i < len; i++) {
        // Wait until ITM port 0 is ready
        while ((ITM->PORT[0].u32 & 1) == 0) { }
        // Write the character (ITM_SendChar() in CMSIS does the same)
        ITM->PORT[0].u8 = (uint8_t)ptr[i];
    }
    return len;
}

// Now printf("hello, ITM\n") goes out via SWO.

ITM also generates timestamp packets — a hardware-stamped time on every packet, useful for measuring how long things actually take instead of guessing.

DWT — Data Watchpoint and Trace

The DWT block at 0xE0001000 provides:

  • Hardware watchpoints — up to 4 (M3/M4/M7) or 8 (M33+). A watchpoint can fire when a specific address is read, written, or accessed at all, optionally with a data-value match. Indispensable for "who is corrupting this variable?" debugging.
  • CYCCNT — cycle counter. A free-running 32-bit counter that ticks every CPU cycle. Used for cycle-accurate profiling: read it, do the thing, read it again, subtract. Wraps every ~28 seconds at 150 MHz.
  • Function profiling counters — count exception entries, sleeps, folded instructions, LSU stalls.
  • PC sampler — periodically reports the PC value. Used for sampling profilers at the system level.
// Enable CYCCNT and measure how long a function takes
CoreDebug->DEMCR |= CoreDebug_DEMCR_TRCENA_Msk;
DWT->CYCCNT = 0;
DWT->CTRL  |= DWT_CTRL_CYCCNTENA_Msk;

uint32_t t0 = DWT->CYCCNT;
expensive_function();
uint32_t t1 = DWT->CYCCNT;

uint32_t cycles = t1 - t0;        // wraps cleanly modulo 2**32
uint32_t microseconds = cycles / (SystemCoreClock / 1000000);

FPB — Flash Patch and Breakpoint

The FPB at 0xE0002000 implements hardware breakpoints (M3/M4: 6 comparators; M7: 8; M33+: configurable, typically 4–8). The breakpoint comparator watches the program counter; when it equals the configured address, the core halts. Hardware breakpoints don't modify the code in flash (unlike software breakpoints, which patch a BKPT instruction in), so they work in code that's executing-from-flash and code in non-rewritable ROM.

The "Flash Patch" half lets you patch a few instructions in ROM by redirecting fetches to an alternative SRAM location. Mostly used for chip-vendor errata workarounds; rarely in user firmware.

ETM — Embedded Trace Macrocell

ETM is the heavyweight: full instruction trace. Every instruction the core executes generates a tiny trace packet, compressed and shipped out via the TPIU (Trace Port Interface Unit) on either SWO (low bandwidth) or a parallel trace port (4-bit @ tens of MHz, hundreds of Mbps). With instruction trace, you can reconstruct the exact PC history leading up to a fault — not just "we're here", but "we got here from here from here, and the heap allocator looked like this on the way in".

ETM is optional and not present on every Cortex-M chip. M3 and M4 designs often omit it for area reasons. M7 and M33 often include it. The RP2350B's M33 cores include ETM.

SWO — Serial Wire Output

SWO is a single chip pin that carries trace data (ITM and/or low-bandwidth ETM) as an asynchronous UART or Manchester-encoded stream out to the probe. SWO + SWD = three pins total (SWCLK + SWDIO + SWO) for a complete debug + trace setup, vs. JTAG's 5+. edevkit1 captures SWO via a PIO state machine on PIO0 SM1 and forwards the stream to the host as part of the CMSIS-DAP v2 USB endpoint — see edevkit1.html §6.1.

The 10-pin Cortex Debug Connector

Arm publishes a standard pinout for the debug header. The "10-pin Cortex Debug Connector" (1.27 mm pitch, sometimes called the "0.05 inch SWD header") is the de-facto standard on every Cortex-M development board.

      Cortex Debug Connector (10-pin, 1.27 mm)
       ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
   1 → │ Vtgt          │ SWDIO / TMS         │ ← 2
   3 → │ GND           │ SWCLK / TCK         │ ← 4
   5 → │ GND           │ SWO    / TDO        │ ← 6
   7 → │ KEY (no pin)  │ NC     / TDI        │ ← 8

"Vtgt" is sensed by the probe so it can level-shift to match the target's IO voltage — this is why edevkit1 has separate Vtgt and Vio domains (see CLAUDE.md gotcha #3). "GNDDetect" lets the probe know the target is connected.

How a host debugger talks to the chip

End-to-end, when you type probe-rs run or click "step over" in your IDE, here's what happens:

  1. Host tool sends a USB request to the probe (CMSIS-DAP, J-Link, ST-Link, BlackMagic — same idea, different protocol).
  2. Probe receives "read word at 0xE000ED30" (a DHCSR access).
  3. Probe encodes the request as SWD bits and clocks them on SWCLK/SWDIO.
  4. The chip's SW-DP receives the request, hands it to the AHB-AP.
  5. AHB-AP issues a 32-bit AHB read to 0xE000ED30 — that's a register inside the SCB.
  6. The result comes back through AHB-AP → SW-DP → probe → USB → host tool.
  7. Host tool interprets the value as the Debug Halting Control / Status register, sees that the core is halted, displays "Stopped at line 42" in your editor.

Step-over, breakpoint set, watch-update, memory dump, all work the same way — they're combinations of "read register X" and "write register Y" through the DAP. Knowing this is what lets you write your own probe (which is what edevkit1 does) or debug a probe that's misbehaving.

Sources: CMSIS-DAP overview, CoreSight Architecture overview, Joseph Yiu, The Definitive Guide to ARM Cortex-M3 and Cortex-M4 Processors (3rd ed., 2014) — chapter 14 covers the debug system in depth.

Power management — sleep modes, WFI, WFE

Battery-life-sensitive applications spend most of their wall-clock time not running code. Cortex-M defines two architectural sleep states; vendor SoCs add their own deeper states layered on top. Knowing the architectural ones is enough to write portable low-power firmware; the SoC-specific ones (STOP / STANDBY / SHUTDOWN on STM32, dormant on RP2350, etc.) live in vendor reference manuals.

Sleep and Deep Sleep

StateTriggerWhat stopsWake-up
SleepWFI or WFE with SLEEPDEEP=0Core clock gated; bus and peripherals continue to run.Any unmasked interrupt; any event for WFE.
Deep SleepWFI/WFE with SLEEPDEEP=1Core + (optionally) PLLs, oscillators, regulators — vendor-specific.Configured wake-up sources (RTC, GPIO, etc.); usually slower wake than Sleep.

The SLEEPDEEP bit lives in SCB->SCR and is cleared by default. The actual depth of "Deep Sleep" — what gets gated, what stays alive — is a vendor decision; ARM only specifies that the core can request "go deeper than Sleep" and the rest of the system honors it.

WFI vs WFE

  • WFI — Wait For Interrupt. Halt the core until an interrupt is pending. The pending interrupt then either (a) wakes the core and fires its handler if unmasked; or (b) wakes the core and resumes after the WFI without firing the handler if masked (PRIMASK=1 etc. — useful for "wake up but don't yet service it"). Most "go to sleep" idle loops use WFI.
  • WFE — Wait For Event. Halt until an event is observed. Events are generated by SEV (set event) instructions on any core (so multi-core cores can wake each other), by external RXEV signals, and (if SEVONPEND is set) by interrupt-pending transitions. Useful for low-overhead spinlock / mailbox patterns between cores.
// The simplest battery-saving idle loop
while (1) {
    if (work_queue_empty()) {
        __WFI();           // sleep until any IRQ wakes us
    } else {
        process_work();
    }
}

SLEEPONEXIT

If you set SCB->SCR.SLEEPONEXIT, the core automatically enters Sleep when it returns from an exception to Thread mode and the exception was the only thing keeping it awake. This is the canonical pattern for "interrupt-driven, sleep-otherwise" code: main() initialises, then enters an infinite WFI; every IRQ wakes briefly, runs, returns, and the core goes back to sleep without an explicit WFI in the main loop.

int main(void) {
    SystemInit();
    init_peripherals();
    SCB->SCR |= SCB_SCR_SLEEPONEXIT_Msk;   // sleep on exception return
    __WFI();                               // first sleep
    // never returns; ISRs do all the work
}

SEVONPEND

SCB->SCR.SEVONPEND makes any new pending interrupt — even masked ones — generate a wake event. Useful for a thread that wants to wake even on masked interrupts (e.g. it wants to peek at the IRQ then re-enable masking).

CMSIS — Cortex Microcontroller Software Interface Standard

CMSIS is Arm's "thin standard library for Cortex-M". It is not a HAL; it does not abstract any peripherals. It provides:

  • CMSIS-Core — typedefs and inline functions for accessing core hardware: NVIC, SysTick, SCB, MPU, ITM, DWT, FPB. Files like core_cm33.h, core_cm0.h, etc. Every Cortex-M project includes CMSIS-Core, directly or indirectly.
  • CMSIS-Driver — abstract C API for common peripherals (USART, SPI, I2C, USB). Standardised but rarely used directly; vendor HALs usually provide their own.
  • CMSIS-DSP — ARM's reference DSP library (FFTs, FIR/IIR, matrix math). Hand-tuned for M4/M7 with the DSP extensions; runnable on M0/M0+ at slower speed.
  • CMSIS-NN — neural-network kernels for keyword spotting, image classification on M-class hardware. Heavily used in TinyML.
  • CMSIS-RTOS2 — a thin standard wrapper over RTOS APIs. Less common than direct RTOS APIs; some safety-critical projects use it for portability.
  • CMSIS-Pack / CMSIS-DAP — packaging and debug-probe interface specs (orthogonal to CMSIS-Core).

CMSIS source lives at github.com/ARM-software/CMSIS_6. Every chip vendor ships a device support pack on top: stm32f4xx.h, nrf52840.h, RP2350.h — these define the vendor's peripheral struct layouts as volatile structs that you read/write from C.

Startup code — what happens between reset and main()

Every Cortex-M firmware needs four things at the bottom: a vector table, a reset handler, a linker script, and a startup file that ties them together. Most vendor SDKs provide one (startup_stm32f407.s, etc.); Zephyr provides one too. But you should know what's inside.

/* Reset handler skeleton — gcc-arm-none-eabi assembly */
    .syntax unified
    .cpu    cortex-m33
    .thumb

    .section .isr_vector, "a"
    .word   _stack_top              /* 0x00: initial MSP            */
    .word   Reset_Handler           /* 0x04: reset                  */
    .word   NMI_Handler             /* 0x08                          */
    .word   HardFault_Handler       /* 0x0C                          */
    /* ... rest of vectors ...                                       */

    .section .text.Reset_Handler, "ax"
    .global Reset_Handler
    .type   Reset_Handler, %function
Reset_Handler:
    /* 1. Set up the stack — already done by hardware loading MSP */

    /* 2. Copy .data from flash LMA to SRAM VMA */
    ldr     r0, =_data_start
    ldr     r1, =_data_end
    ldr     r2, =_data_lma
1:  cmp     r0, r1
    beq     2f
    ldr     r3, [r2], #4
    str     r3, [r0], #4
    b       1b

    /* 3. Zero .bss */
2:  ldr     r0, =_bss_start
    ldr     r1, =_bss_end
    movs    r2, #0
3:  cmp     r0, r1
    beq     4f
    str     r2, [r0], #4
    b       3b

    /* 4. Optional: enable FPU (set CP10/CP11 full access in CPACR) */
4:  ldr     r0, =0xE000ED88
    ldr     r1, [r0]
    orr     r1, r1, #(0xF << 20)
    str     r1, [r0]
    dsb
    isb

    /* 5. Call vendor's SystemInit() (clocks, voltage, flash latency) */
    bl      SystemInit

    /* 6. Run C++ static constructors (not used on bare-metal C) */
    bl      __libc_init_array

    /* 7. Jump to main */
    bl      main

    /* 8. main() returned — spin forever */
5:  b       5b
    .size   Reset_Handler, . - Reset_Handler

Linker script anatomy

The linker script tells the linker where each section ends up. Minimal Cortex-M skeleton:

/* Memory regions */
MEMORY
{
    FLASH (rx)  : ORIGIN = 0x10000000, LENGTH = 2M
    SRAM  (rwx) : ORIGIN = 0x20000000, LENGTH = 520K
}

ENTRY(Reset_Handler)

SECTIONS
{
    .isr_vector : {
        KEEP(*(.isr_vector))    /* place vector table at flash base */
    } > FLASH

    .text : {
        *(.text*)               /* code              */
        *(.rodata*)             /* read-only data    */
        _etext = .;             /* end of code/rodata */
    } > FLASH

    /* .data: lives in SRAM at runtime, but is *loaded* from flash. */
    .data : AT(_etext) {
        _data_start = .;
        *(.data*)
        _data_end = .;
    } > SRAM
    _data_lma = LOADADDR(.data);

    .bss : {
        _bss_start = .;
        *(.bss*)
        *(COMMON)
        _bss_end = .;
    } > SRAM

    /* Stack grows downward from the top of SRAM */
    _stack_top = ORIGIN(SRAM) + LENGTH(SRAM);
}

Three concepts to internalise here:

  1. VMA vs LMA. VMA (virtual / link-time address) is where a section lives at runtime. LMA (load address) is where it's stored in the binary. .data lives in SRAM (VMA = 0x20000000…) but is stored in flash after .text (LMA = end of flash). The reset handler copies LMA → VMA.
  2. .bss is in the binary as a size, not as content. The linker just records "0x800 bytes of bss starting at 0x20003000"; the reset handler fills it with zeros. This is why static int x[1000]; doesn't bloat your firmware image.
  3. Stack grows down from the top of SRAM. The vector table's slot 0 holds _stack_top, which the linker computes as ORIGIN(SRAM) + LENGTH(SRAM). The hardware loads MSP from there before the first instruction runs.

Build systems

Three options dominate Cortex-M land:

  • CMake. The default for Zephyr (which generates a CMake project per board). Cross-platform, well-supported by every IDE.
  • Make. Many vendor SDKs and tutorial projects ship a Makefile. Simple, hackable; struggles at scale.
  • Meson. Used by some newer projects (e.g. some PX4 components). Faster than CMake but smaller embedded ecosystem.

RTT — Real-Time Transfer

An aside that's worth knowing: RTT is a Segger-invented protocol (now widely supported) that uses two ring buffers in target SRAM as a bidirectional communication channel with the probe. The probe polls the buffers via SWD reads and writes; firmware just reads/writes them like any RAM. RTT is faster than ITM/SWO, full-duplex, and works without dedicating a chip pin. probe-rs and J-Link both speak it; many embedded shops use RTT instead of UART for log output.

Hello, ARM — a complete walkthrough

The fastest way to internalise the previous 1500 lines is to build a working firmware from scratch — startup file, linker script, main() that toggles a GPIO — and flash it. We'll target the Raspberry Pi Pico 2 (RP2350) because it's the same silicon edevkit1 uses and you can buy one for ~$5. The walkthrough also works with minor changes for STM32 Nucleo or any other Cortex-M devkit.

prerequisites Follow installation.html first to get arm-none-eabi-gcc, probe-rs, and a Pi Pico Debug Probe (or another edevkit) on your machine. We assume those exist for the rest of this section.

main.c — toggle the on-board LED

The Pi Pico 2's on-board LED is on GPIO25. To toggle it we (1) enable the GPIO pad, (2) set the function-select to "SIO" (single-cycle IO), (3) toggle the SIO output register.

/* hello-arm/main.c */
#include <stdint.h>

#define IO_BANK0_BASE  0x40028000UL
#define PADS_BANK0_BASE 0x40038000UL
#define SIO_BASE       0xD0000000UL
#define RESETS_BASE    0x40020000UL

#define GPIO25_CTRL  (*(volatile uint32_t*)(IO_BANK0_BASE + 0x0CC))
#define GPIO25_PAD   (*(volatile uint32_t*)(PADS_BANK0_BASE + 0x68))
#define GPIO_OE_SET  (*(volatile uint32_t*)(SIO_BASE + 0x024))
#define GPIO_OUT_XOR (*(volatile uint32_t*)(SIO_BASE + 0x01C))
#define RESETS_RESET (*(volatile uint32_t*)(RESETS_BASE + 0x00))

static void delay(volatile uint32_t n) {
    while (n--) __asm volatile ("nop");
}

int main(void) {
    /* Bring IO_BANK0 and PADS_BANK0 out of reset */
    RESETS_RESET &= ~((1u << 5) | (1u << 8));
    while (RESETS_RESET & ((1u << 5) | (1u << 8))) { /* wait */ }

    /* Function select 5 = SIO; clear pad isolation */
    GPIO25_CTRL = 5;
    GPIO25_PAD  = 0;

    /* Enable output on GPIO25 */
    GPIO_OE_SET = (1u << 25);

    /* Blink */
    while (1) {
        GPIO_OUT_XOR = (1u << 25);
        delay(2000000);
    }
}

Notice what's not in this file: no #includes from a vendor SDK, no CMSIS, no startup setup. We're talking directly to RP2350's IO_BANK / PADS_BANK / SIO peripherals because we want to see every layer.

startup.s — vector table and reset handler

/* hello-arm/startup.s — minimal RP2350 reset for Cortex-M33 core 0 */
    .syntax unified
    .cpu    cortex-m33
    .thumb

    .section .isr_vector, "a", %progbits
    .word   _stack_top              /* 0: initial MSP */
    .word   Reset_Handler           /* 1: reset       */
    .word   Default_Handler         /* 2: NMI         */
    .word   Default_Handler         /* 3: HardFault   */
    /* (rest left as Default_Handler — see real vendor file for full list) */

    .section .text.Reset_Handler, "ax", %progbits
    .global Reset_Handler
    .type   Reset_Handler, %function
Reset_Handler:
    /* Copy .data from flash (LMA) to SRAM (VMA) */
    ldr     r0, =_data_start
    ldr     r1, =_data_end
    ldr     r2, =_data_lma
1:  cmp     r0, r1
    bge     2f
    ldr     r3, [r2], #4
    str     r3, [r0], #4
    b       1b

    /* Zero .bss */
2:  ldr     r0, =_bss_start
    ldr     r1, =_bss_end
    movs    r3, #0
3:  cmp     r0, r1
    bge     4f
    str     r3, [r0], #4
    b       3b

    /* Jump to main */
4:  bl      main
    /* If main() returns, hang */
5:  b       5b

    .global Default_Handler
    .type   Default_Handler, %function
Default_Handler:
    b       Default_Handler

link.ld — linker script

/* hello-arm/link.ld — RP2350 XIP flash + SRAM */
MEMORY
{
    FLASH (rx)  : ORIGIN = 0x10000100, LENGTH = 2M - 0x100
    SRAM  (rwx) : ORIGIN = 0x20000000, LENGTH = 520K
}

ENTRY(Reset_Handler)

SECTIONS
{
    .isr_vector : {
        KEEP(*(.isr_vector))
    } > FLASH

    .text : {
        *(.text*)
        *(.rodata*)
        _etext = .;
    } > FLASH

    .data : AT(_etext) {
        _data_start = .;
        *(.data*)
        _data_end = .;
    } > SRAM
    _data_lma = LOADADDR(.data);

    .bss : {
        _bss_start = .;
        *(.bss*)
        *(COMMON)
        _bss_end = .;
    } > SRAM

    _stack_top = ORIGIN(SRAM) + LENGTH(SRAM);
}
Real RP2350 boot RP2350 needs a "Stage-2 boot" block at 0x10000000 to configure the QSPI flash before XIP can run, plus a metadata "image definition" block. We're cheating in this minimal example by starting at 0x10000100 (after the boot2 region) and assuming a probe loaded the real boot2 already, or that we're writing into RAM. For a real flashable image, see the RP2350 datasheet chapter 5 ("Bootrom") and the picotool-generated headers. This walkthrough is for understanding the layers, not for production.

Makefile

# hello-arm/Makefile
TARGET   := hello-arm

            -Os -g -ffunction-sections -fdata-sections \
            -Wall -Wextra -std=c11 -nostdlib

LDFLAGS  := -mcpu=cortex-m33 -mthumb -mfloat-abi=hard -mfpu=fpv5-sp-d16 \
            -nostdlib -nostartfiles -T link.ld -Wl,--gc-sections \
            -Wl,-Map=$(TARGET).map

all: $(TARGET).elf $(TARGET).bin
	$(SIZE) $(TARGET).elf

%.o: %.s
	$(CC) $(CFLAGS) -c $< -o $@

%.o: %.c
	$(CC) $(CFLAGS) -c $< -o $@

$(TARGET).elf: $(OBJS)
	$(LD) $(LDFLAGS) $^ -o $@

What to do next

  1. Add a SysTick interrupt in place of the busy-wait delay(). You'll need to populate the SysTick slot in the vector table and write a handler. Easy.
  2. Use CMSIS-Core by including core_cm33.h and the RP2350 device-support header. Watch the build size grow ~200 bytes; in return you get NVIC_EnableIRQ(), __WFI(), etc.
  3. Switch to Zephyr. Read installation.html, set up a west workspace, and run west build -b rpi_pico2 zephyr/samples/basic/blinky. You'll see the same blinking LED, but the project is now using a full RTOS, device-tree-driven drivers, and a tested USB stack — at the cost of ~50 KB of binary.
